Demographics on Riker's Island 2015 vs. 2021

Byline: Ana Teresa Solá

October 7, 2021

"We have to end this torture. This place is a slaughterhouse. Rikers jail does not deserve to be operating today. Those human beings are dying [...] They don't even treat animals the way they treat these people! Even though you are sentenced to confinement, you deserve to be alive and we have to return these people to their homes." - Melania Brown, Ralley against Riker's Island, September 2021

After 10 deaths in the past nine months in prison on Rikers Island, several organizations that advocate for prison reform on Wednesday called for an end to the systemic abuse perpetuated by New York City public officials and demanded an appropriate investment to help underserved communities thrive. During a ralley in front of the New York City Hall, the director of the Freedom Agenda organization, Brandon Holmes, demanded that Mayor Bill de Blasio release the 6,082 confined to Rikers and activate Law 6-8, which would allow the use of alternate confinement spaces so that detainees could continue their court hearings and remain in contact with their community. “It has been invested in these options before, like when the city faced the COVID-19 crisis at its peak. We are still in crisis now, but this administration does not want to recognize it because in this case, it is poor and Black people who are dying," said Holmes during his speech. Indeed, the majority of people who go in and rarely come out of Riker's Island are Black people.

In 2015, only 26% Black, 36% Asian and 24% people categorized as Other were incarcerated within the year of 2015.

60% of the Black incarcerated citizens were male and 26% female.

In comparison, in 2021, there are 71,645 Black people registered in the system, alongside 35,422 categorized as Other and 14,146 individuals as White.

Amongst the Black incarcerated, 117,190 are registered as male and 6,386 as female.
